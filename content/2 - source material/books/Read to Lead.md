### Summary:

If you wish to push beyond the frontier of what you are currently capable of, you must read and learn. Reading and learning is the highest ROI activity you can do right now. 
### Key takeaways:
reading reduces [[knowledge debt]] which is an anchor to an progress you make. The goal is to reduce this knowledge however you can, most ROI option is books.

>"As President Harry S. Truman said, 'Not all readers are leaders, but all leaders are readers'"

My first reaction was to bat against this as I know plenty of managers who don't read. But then again those are just managers. Being a manager does not inherently make you a leader. Being a manager is having the authority to tell people what to do. Being a leader is to get them to follow you to what you want them to do. So, they want to follow. Is this quote true today? Perhaps, but I know the statistical likelihood of a great leader being a reader is not higher than a non-reader.

>"You will be the same person in five years as you are today except for the people you meet and the books you read. " - Charlie "Tremendous" Jones

New experiences are touted as the ultimate eye opener. But if the lens with which we experience new things never changes how can we expect our insights from those new experiences to have be more than if they were not? And so, its not the actually new experience that brings benefits, but the insight shift that happens because of it. A new lens. Well, that lens can be more readily and easily changed when you can purposefully explore, in depth, a new perspective that someone has spent hundreds of pages writing about.

Kahneman talks about this in ch.7 of [[Thinking, Fast and Slow]], [[system 1]]  jumps to conclusions to fill gaps about ambiguity. System 1, though it jumps to conclusions will never question if its jump is valid because system 1 does not deal with doubt and correctness.

53
>"If you were the average English speaking person living in the seventeenth century, and you picked up today's edition of the *New York Times*, you would read more information in one issue than you would come across in your lifetime."

Holy shit. One must imagine how well our ape brains are doing with this massive flux of information constantly bombarding our little heads. I mean really, are we truly going to be ok in the long run with this kind of intake happening all the time? The trends we see with the rise of social media usage and the growing decline of cognitive markers like literacy rates, mental health decline, etc. are exactly what I would expect to see with a brain thats constantly being pushed to it's bandwidth limit on the intake side. Now, what happens if we were to cut that intake down to an intentional amount, and redirect all remaining brain output that would have been spent filtering information, into now working with that information and synthesizing it. Which leads me to wonder. If we are constantly bombarding ourselves with intake, is that taking away from the brains total capacity to perform other activities such as synthesis and creation? Does the brain operate in a 'bandwidth way' where there only so much capacity and when you overload one region, the brain has to draw resources from another?

>"If we encounter a man of rare intellect, we should ask him what books he reads" - Ralph Waldo Emerson

Yes obviously smart people read books, but its more than that. Smart people are shaped by great thinkers that came before them. They iterate and build upon knowledge that was given to them, shaped by their own experiences and formed to be apart of something greater. And by coming to the foundation how they built their knowledge, their thinking systems we can use that source as a good source of knowledge to integrate into our own life.

93
>"Employ your time in improving yourself by other men's writings so that you shall come easily by what others have labored hard for" - Socrates

Human advancement looks like this. exploring the frontier -> explorers of the frontier pass on knowledge-> settling the frontier -> -> frontier moves forward -> old frontier becomes intermediate topic -> repeat into eternity ([[settling the frontier and human advancement]]). So if humankind advancement looks like that, why cant the individual man replicate the same process within himself? Let the experts tell you what they discovered on the frontier, so that you don't waste time charting already known territory, you can keep pushing on, settling new frontiers for yourself. With the experiences of the new frontier in mind, then you can go back and iterate on your old charting of already known territory and see if its still acceptable to you.

161
>"We are what we repeatedly do. Excellence then, is not an act, but a habit." - Will Durant

The quotes in this book are phenomenal. I guess if you really want people to listen to your point its wise to quote men who make good points. But, on to the point. Excellence then is not a one off by product of some action. Its a constant. Extraordinary people do extraordinary things. Do they even recognize that what they are doing is extraordinary? That's something that I have been thinking on for quite a bit. I wage on the side of probably not. If you are to repeat extraordinary everyday, its not extraordinary to you, its normal. Does the power of extraordinary come from a single explosion or does the culminations of consistent 'greats' become the extraordinary thing?
