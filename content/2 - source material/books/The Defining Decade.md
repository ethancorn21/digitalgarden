### Summary:
Try in your 20's so you don't have to struggle in your 30's. We often see 20's as a period of frolicking and time that is ok to be wasted. But in reality it is arguably the most important period of your life. 
### Key takeaways:
Didn't get far into the book because it wasn't all that interesting and was written for people who need a gentler approach to actually trying in life, compared to what I am used to too, which is high pressure, do or die mentality.

> "identity capital is our stock of personal assets. It is how we add value to who we are, and it is what we have to show for how we have spent our time...some identity capital-such as degrees or jobs test scores or clubs-goes on a resume. Other identity capital is more personal. It is how we present ourselves, where we happen to be from, how we solve problems, or what our hobbies or life experience may be...It is the currency we use to metaphorically purchase jobs and opportunities and other things we want."

This is an interesting thing to think about because on one hand I don't like the commoditization of experiences as something to do to benefit career trajectory. On the other, I feel it is all I naturally do. To me, every action has to fit into the larger strategy of life. Whether that is the right approach, probably not, but it is one I have configured for myself. I am also reminded of social capital. The idea that we accumulate "favors" through our deeds and words such that we can "spend" social capital with others in favor of what we want. Personal capital to me just sounds like social capital with a cool name. Is actions and words done in the name of experience to be socially talked about later not exactly that, social capital.

13
> "you twenties are the time for that cross-country job or that graduate degree or that start-up you want to try" 

My take away from this that has been confirmed by numerous successful people is that you must take risks. Risks when you are young are exponentially more advantageous because 1) you have little to lose 2) it gives you a step above in terms of learning over those who have not dared to lose. So take risks, especially in your 20's because they are largely asymmetric. Stop looking at life as a rigid thing that must move in a progressive pattern that fits into your world view. Instead begin to see your life as an organic material that is not locked into place but is free to be shaped however you so choose.

> "He that hath once done you a kindness will be more ready to do you another than he whom you yourself have obliged" - Benjamin Franklin

This is the [[Ben Franklin Effect]], basically you are more willing to help someone who has asked you for help than someone who has helped you. I figure this is why so many people say "he needs to remember where he came from". It is also interesting that we have this inherent desire to please a new stimulus rather than pay back a known one. 
I remember listening to Alex Hormozi talk about asking for help. He said most people are willing to help you and give you advice, even people who you think are way above you in social strata, but only if they think you are going to act on their help / advice.
