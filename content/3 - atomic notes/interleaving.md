# interleaving
The process of working on / solving problems from two or more topics at the same time. Interleaving increases your ability to discriminate between two topics, meaning that your ability to discern the underlying principles of what makes a specific problem *what* is more obvious to you.

### examples:
When solving math problems, randomize order in which you solve different type of problems. Use formula A, then Formula D, then Formula B, Then Formula A, then Formula B, then Formula C, etc.

When reading, read 2-5 books at a time. This will help you draw parallels in their themes and commonalities, as well as their differences. Useful for reading books within the same field or even connecting completely separate books. (Syntopic Reading?)
ex: 
reading [[Make it Stick]] and [[The Autobiography of Benjamin Franklin]], and connecting that Benjamin Franklin wrote from memory ([[testing effect]]) the works of better writers, so that he could see the difference between his wording and the best writer. Allowing him to rapidly improve and become a well remembered writer ([[deliberate practice]]).
