[[psychology]], [[thinking traps]]

When we have to estimate the time to do something, we often suck at it. We almost always grossly underestimate the amount of time it takes to do something. Why? Could it be the pressure to achieve a specific goal by a deadline that pushes us to make optimistic estimations? If this is true, why then do we still make terrible guesses for time even when we are asked for the worst case scenario?

The problem lies fundamentally in the act of estimating time. We are converting from one measurement to another unit of measurement. And its in this conversion that we often lose the plot. For example say you have a spreadsheet that needs to be done. You have a bunch of microtasks to complete inside the spreadsheet for it to be deemed finished. We then take these microtasks, estimate how long each will take and then add up all the time estimations for the microtass to get how long the spreadsheet will take. Do you see the inherent problem there? We are taking one unit of measurement, work that needs to be done, and converting it to another unit of measurement, time. These two units of measurement have nothing to do with each other though. Sure, it takes time to do work, but it doesn't take work to do time. They aren't cross compatible with each other. It's like saying, "this project will take me 3 Ticonderoga pencils to finish". You might be saying, "well wait a second that's kind of a drastic leap from time to Ticonderoga pencils as a unit of progress". Is it? Why?
