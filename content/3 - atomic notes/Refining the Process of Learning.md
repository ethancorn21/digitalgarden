I don't think anybody is inherently a good learner. We are raised with structured learning. Given the material we need to memorized, then at a snails pace, we slowly move towards fulfilling the learning goals set by the school district. For many people, and dare I say *most* people, structured learning like this completely kills the inherent curiosity in many people. But the problem isn't learning, it's school. Nobody hates learning. We are evolutionarily adapted to learn new useful things without much effort. It's when we can't directly measure the exact *n* units of effort it takes to get *n* units of result that we struggle to apply ourselves to the knowledge at hand. Ask yourself, what makes something hard to a beginner and easy to a master?

The answer lies in context. The master has thousands of hours of learning to be able to tie their knowledge into. What does the beginner have? The master can tie new things they have learned into the foundation they have built for themselves. Foundation. That's where the problem lies. The beginner has no foundation. So when we go to learn something hard, it is solely hard because we don't have the foundational knowledge as a prerequisite to understand what is trying to be conveyed. 

This leads us to a solution. When we are struggling with understanding something. Truly struggling, we have no clue where to start. We have to step back from the problem in front of us and devolve to the components that make it up. When in doubt regress to the basics.
