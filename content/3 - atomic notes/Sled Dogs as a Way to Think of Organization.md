# Sled Dogs as a Way to Think of Organization

The act of a team of dogs pulling a vehicle/payload, called mushing, is thought to be over 9,500 years old [1]. What can we learn from this?

I first wish to draw parallels between mushing and the goal of a company. In mushing, you have a team of dogs pulling a payload across varied terrain in a turbulent environment. The dogs are organized into a hierarchical structure that allows the driver, musher, to "drive" the sled to where they need to go.

![[Mushing_graphicx.jpg\ |600]]

Is this not all to dissimilar to a company? 
The superstars of the companies, ones that you do not have to push to achieve excellence, these are your wheel dogs. You want to place them as close to your payload as possible. They will bear the brunt of the weight happily.

You have the individual contributors, the coders, designers, engineers, etc. pulling the mule's burden of the weight. We can think of these guys as the team dogs. They make up the bulk of the organization. Coasting along, supported by the superstars and directed by the swing dogs.

Next, you have the managers. They will ensure the team and wheel dogs are progressing into the right direction. It's here the big picture begins to be seen. The managers are the swing dogs. They understand that a pivot is needed, they don't know exactly how, but they know it is.

At the lead, the C suite. These are the drivers right hand confidants. They are expected to see the changing landscape and react accordingly to what they believe is right. But, they are only dogs. They don't have the big picture as the driver should. They don't have the power of the wheel dogs. So, they wield influence to drive and originate their ideas. First on, last off. They keep the rest of the team in check and will always be listening for more commands.
![[500px-Alaska_musher_in_1909.jpg\ |200]]
Finally, the driver. These are your dreamers. Ideally, the owner / CEO should be the dreamer. They can monitor the wheel dogs, team dogs, swing dogs, lead dogs. They can see and monitor the whole org. Yet, their influence is not at the bottom, it is at the top. And so, to react to a change that needs to happen at the bottom of the org, they have to work the lead dogs, which will work the swing dogs, which will work the team dogs, which will then finally trickle down to the wheel dogs. They see the landscape, they know where they are going, and they know how to work the organization to achieve their goals.

The problem as I see it. Many people think being a leader means being the lead dog. They equate being down in the mud, making the quick needed decisions is what they need. But, if your leader is down in the mud making reactionary decisions, who is seeing the 40 thousand foot view? But, another problem comes when you only look at the 40,000 ft view and never at your wheel dogs. So, in a gist, keep an eye on your bottom line to see the foundational health of the org. Use your lead dogs to drive the other dogs, not your payload.
