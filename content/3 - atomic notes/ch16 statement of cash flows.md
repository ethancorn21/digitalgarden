# ch16 statement of cash flows

cash flows reconciles the difference between profit and the amount of cash a company actually has. Because of accrual based accounting, counting sales as revenue before being payed, the cash often looks different than what should be in there. 

### where did the cash go:
- operating - any expense (interest included)
- investing - 
- financing - anything w/ debt or shareholder equity

Indirect method:
- step 1 : add things back to SCF that reduced profit (but isn't cash)(ie. depreciation expense)
- step 2 : what changed on the balance sheet (assets, equities, liabilities)

An increase in liabilities is added back to net income because that money hasn't actually left your hands yet. The loaner is essentially holding an IOU and you are holding the cash that can be spent else where. It is added back because in accrual based accounting whenever you add an entry for account payable, you credit cash. But in reality you still hold that cash. So to better reflect reality, which a statement of cash flows is intended to do. You would add that back to net income because it hasn't actually outflowed anywhere.
An increase in inventory had to have been bought with money. So you will subtract it from net income because there was an outflow of money.

The three steps to figure out the cash provided or used by investment activities:
1) identify changes
	- what changed?
	- look at balance sheet and see how long term investment changed
2) reconstruct using T - Accounts
	- the 'why' behind the what
3) report effects
	- write down cash amounts in the investing section

An increase in Notes Payable means that the financing Section will increase by that amount
- the company takes on more debt, it owes more money. But they also received cash. 
- Notes Payable = a loan. The company now has a liability, but its cash increases, so an increase in Notes Payable = an addition to the Financing Activities because there is cash inflow.
